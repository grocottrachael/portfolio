Limpid Markets

A product used by the world's major banks to trade precious metal derivatives

The brief

Precious metal derivatives have traditionally been arranged via a voice broker acting as a middleman between traders to arrange deals. But a new generation of traders have become used to agreeing deals using online platforms which removes the need for a broker and reduces fees, whilst providing greater transparency. Up until Limpid Markets, there’s been no such platform for precious metal derivatives.

This is a product that UVD have worked on since the startup launched six years ago, therefore a lot of my role has been to make ongoing improvements to the user experience and design, as well as adding new products to the platform.

Understanding

Once we’ve worked with Limpid Markets’ founder to establish the next highest priority improvement, we’ll begin an impact mapping exercise to establish ways we can solve the problem. From there, we’ll write a script of questions to ask the users.

These are busy, time poor traders who have high expectations from the product, so when speaking to them, we have to be sure we’re asking the right questions to make the best use of their time.

After speaking to the traders we add a summary of the interview to Airtable which can be distrubuted amonst our team, as well as with Limpid Markets’ founder.

UX improvements

One thing I really wanted to tackle on Limpid Markets was the high level menu items which were hard to understand, but also inconsistent.

  • Clicking the cog opened a modal
  • [bin] orders opened a drop down menu to allow users to remove their orders from the board
  • The alarm clock opened the same drop down but allowed users to add a time that their orders would be automatically removed (obvious, right?!)
  • And the bell actually opened an activity feed

We found that traders would often ask us how to remove their orders at a scheduled time as well as asking us about some more complex settings that were hidden behind the cog. Therefore a good KPI for this improvement was that we'd see less traders asking us how to do certain things, as well as tracking the new feature using Matomo.

Wireframes

When we’re looking for solutions to our probelms, I will always run what we’ve coined a ‘sketching session’. This will involve the Product Owner, and our product team consisting of me, a frontend developer and a backend developer. Bringing everyone together for the early ideas generation not only gives us a much broader range of solutions, but the engineers can tell us at the pen and paper stage what’s feasible.

Limpid Markets’ Product Owner is based in Madrid, so we used a tool called Realtime Board to collate the sketches and to give any feedback.

There were quite a range of ideas sketched out, so we decided to take three versions further. The big comment is the client stipulating which of the designs he likes best, which is surprisingly his own..!

V1

The client much preferred the option of a menu area sliding down from the top of the screen as this would give the most space for any future additions to a menu area.

V2

Another option here was to have a tabbed view, meaning the trading board would get it’s own view, and any settings can be accessed separately and with a lot of real-estate.

V3

This option was more left-field as we tend to heavily advise our desktop clients to never use a hamburger menu, but as the user base is small, and space is limited, we thought we’d give it a try.

The order management funtion was to remain outside of the menu to remove clicks to this often used feature.

Testing

Once we had these three options, the next step was to see which the traders preferred. As this was to test a user journey, I used InVision to create a prototype, and at the time the ‘Live Share’ feature still worked, which was great as I could give the traders control and watch how they used the prototype.

After speaking to around ten traders, it was clear that the hamburger menu was the preferred option, so I was able to flesh out the prototype more and more until we had something that the traders, client and we were happy with.

Final design

Adding the final design touches to the screens allowed me to make a few more improvements to the frontend, such as editing the logo, and also brightening up the colour palette. The end result is something that the client is really happy with, and the clean design and subtle animations on the frontend have received great feedback from the users.
